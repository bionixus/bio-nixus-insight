Detailed analysis

Regulatory Authority Overview

Pharmaceutical registration in Kuwait is governed by the Ministry of Health (MOH) — Drug & Food Control Administration, which serves as the country's primary pharmaceutical regulatory body. The legal framework is established under Ministerial Decree No. 302/2019 (also referenced as Decree 361/2019 in some publications), which mandates that all pharmaceutical products undergo comprehensive review before gaining market authorisation.

The Drug & Food Control Administration oversees several critical functions: drug registration and re-registration, quality control and laboratory testing, pricing approval, pharmacovigilance and adverse event monitoring, and import licensing. Unlike some GCC counterparts, Kuwait's MOH does not currently operate a fully electronic submission system — physical dossiers must be submitted in person through an appointed local agent via prior appointment.

For pharmaceutical companies accustomed to the streamlined digital portals of the SFDA (Saudi Arabia) or MOHAP (UAE), Kuwait's paper-based system represents a process that requires careful planning and local expertise. However, modernisation efforts are underway as part of Kuwait's Vision 2035 digital transformation programme.

Registration Requirements

Kuwait's drug registration process requires comprehensive documentation demonstrating the safety, efficacy, and quality of pharmaceutical products. The key requirements under Ministerial Decree 302/2019 include:

  • Common Technical Document (CTD) — Complete Module 1-5 documentation in CTD format is mandatory for all registration applications
  • Clinical trial data — Including Phase I-III clinical study reports, patient safety data, and efficacy endpoints
  • Bioequivalence studies — Required for all generic pharmaceutical products
  • Stability data — Both accelerated (6-month) and long-term (3-batch) stability studies, with conditions appropriate for Zone IVB climate
  • GMP certificates — From recognised international authorities (FDA, EMA, MHRA, or GCC health authorities)
  • WHO Certificate of Pharmaceutical Product (WHO-CoPP) — Certifying the product's registration status in the country of origin
  • Free Sale Certificate — Evidence the product is freely marketed in the country of origin
  • Product samples — Physical samples of the finished product and reference standards with Certificate of Analysis for MOH laboratory testing
  • Finished product specifications — Complete quality control methods and acceptance criteria
  • Raw material and API specifications — Including supplier qualification documentation
  • Arabic labelling — Bilingual (Arabic/English) labelling on all packaging and patient information leaflets is mandatory
  • Legalised price list — From the country of origin, authenticated through official channels

Local Agent Mandate

One of the most critical requirements for foreign pharmaceutical companies seeking to register products in Kuwait is the mandatory appointment of a local licensed pharmaceutical company as their authorised agent. This is not optional — it is a legal requirement that applies to all foreign manufacturers without exception.

The local agent serves as the company's official representative before the MOH and is responsible for: submitting and managing the registration dossier, handling all correspondence with the Drug & Food Control Administration, managing import permits and customs clearance, providing physical product samples for laboratory testing, maintaining product stock and distribution, and handling pharmacovigilance reporting obligations.

Choosing the right local agent is arguably the most important strategic decision for a pharmaceutical company entering Kuwait. Major distributors such as YIACO Medical (Kuwait Drug Company), Alghanim Industries Healthcare Division, and Warba Pharmaceutical Company have established track records representing international principals. The agent's relationships with MOH officials, warehousing capabilities, and sales force coverage should all factor into the selection process.

CTD Dossier Requirements

Kuwait follows the internationally harmonised Common Technical Document (CTD) format for pharmaceutical registration submissions. The CTD comprises five modules:

  1. Module 1 — Administrative Information: Regional-specific information including application form, proposed labelling, product information, local agent authorisation letter, and pricing documentation. This module is Kuwait-specific and must be tailored to MOH requirements.
  2. Module 2 — Summaries: Quality overall summary, non-clinical overview and written/tabulated summaries, clinical overview and summary. These provide the MOH reviewers with a high-level assessment framework.
  3. Module 3 — Quality: Drug substance (API) and drug product manufacturing, characterisation, control, and stability data. GMP compliance documentation is particularly scrutinised.
  4. Module 4 — Non-Clinical Study Reports: Pharmacology, pharmacokinetics, and toxicology data from pre-clinical studies.
  5. Module 5 — Clinical Study Reports: All clinical trial reports, including pivotal efficacy and safety studies. For generics, bioequivalence study reports are included here.

One important practical consideration: Kuwait MOH currently requires physical hard-copy dossiers rather than electronic CTD (eCTD) submissions. This means companies must prepare printed, bound copies of their complete registration dossiers. The physical submission must be delivered in person by the local agent through a scheduled appointment with the Drug & Food Control Administration.

Registration Timeline

Pharmaceutical registration in Kuwait typically takes 12 to 24 months from initial submission to marketing authorisation, depending on the product type and completeness of the dossier. The process follows several stages:

  1. Pre-submission preparation (1-3 months) — Appoint local agent, prepare and compile CTD dossier, obtain necessary certificates (GMP, CoPP, Free Sale), translate labelling to Arabic, arrange product samples
  2. Dossier submission (1-2 weeks) — Physical submission through local agent via scheduled appointment; receipt of tracking number
  3. Administrative review (1-3 months) — MOH verifies completeness of documentation; deficiency letters issued if information is missing; response deadlines vary
  4. Laboratory testing (2-4 months) — Product samples undergo quality control testing at MOH laboratories; this is a distinctive feature of Kuwait's process compared to some GCC countries
  5. Scientific assessment (3-8 months) — Quality, safety, and efficacy review; may include requests for additional data
  6. Pricing approval (1-3 months) — Cost-plus pricing calculation; manufacturer submits proposed price with legalised price list from country of origin
  7. Marketing authorisation (2-4 weeks) — Registration certificate issued; valid for 5 years; import licence obtained through local agent

Registration certificates must be renewed every 5 years, with renewal applications submitted at least 6 months before expiry to avoid gaps in market authorisation.

Pricing and Approval

Kuwait uses a cost-plus pricing model with a manufacturer price ceiling. The pricing approval process is a distinct phase within the overall registration timeline and requires:

  • Legalised price list from the country of origin, authenticated through the Kuwait Embassy or relevant government channels
  • Ex-factory price justification demonstrating the relationship between manufacturing cost and proposed wholesale price
  • Comparative pricing data from other GCC countries where the product is registered

The government procurement channel (Central Medical Stores) typically drives the lowest prices through competitive tendering. Private sector pricing allows slightly higher margins but is still subject to MOH-approved maximum retail prices. For innovator products, the cost-plus model can be challenging — particularly when compared to value-based pricing approaches used in some Western markets.

Pharmaceutical companies should factor pricing strategy into their overall market access plan early in the registration process. Understanding the balance between government tender pricing and private sector margins is essential for commercial viability.

GCC Centralised Registration

Kuwait participates in the GCC Centralised Registration Procedure, which offers an alternative pathway for pharmaceutical market access. Under this mechanism, a pharmaceutical company can submit a single registration application to one GCC member state, and the approved registration is recognised by other participating countries.

The Gulf Health Council coordinates this process, which aims to reduce duplication of regulatory review across the six GCC states (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain, Oman). However, several important caveats apply:

  • Pricing approval is country-specific — Even with centralised registration, each country retains independent pricing authority
  • Import permits remain national — Local import licensing must be obtained separately in each market
  • Local agent required — A Kuwait-based authorised agent is still mandatory regardless of the registration pathway
  • Adoption varies — Not all product categories are eligible, and the practical implementation varies across member states

For companies with products already registered in Saudi Arabia (SFDA) or the UAE (MOHAP), the centralised procedure may offer a faster route to Kuwait market access. For complete details on GCC-wide market access, see our GCC Pharmaceutical Market Access Guide 2026.

Biologics and Biosimilars

Kuwait permits the registration of biological products and biosimilars, provided they are registered with competent international authorities (EMA or FDA) and meet Gulf Health Council guidelines for biosimilar evaluation. The MOH evaluates biologics with particular attention to:

  • Comparability studies demonstrating similarity to the reference biologic
  • Immunogenicity data and risk management plans
  • Post-marketing surveillance commitments
  • Cold-chain storage and distribution capabilities of the local agent
