Executive Summary
UAE Biosimilars at an Inflection Point

BioNixus sizes the UAE biosimilars market at roughly USD 135 million in 2026, advancing toward about USD 275 million by 2030 at roughly 19.5% CAGR—a band triangulated against Bonafide Research's UAE biosimilars growth outlook (>19.45% CAGR 2025–2030) and Insights10's monoclonal-antibody biosimilar trajectory (USD 35 million in 2022 to USD 175 million by 2030 at 22.3% CAGR for mAbs alone), extended to filgrastim, pegfilgrastim, and other supportive-care molecules.

The UAE sits inside a USD 4.45 billion pharmaceutical market (IMARC Group, 2025)—a premium, insurance-driven ecosystem where biologics command disproportionate spend in immunology, oncology, and supportive care. Biosimilar substitution is accelerating, but through a dual-channel architecture unlike Saudi Arabia's NUPCO national tender: Abu Dhabi government facilities operate under DOH policy mandates and SEHA inpatient rules, while Dubai and the northern emirates substitute through insurer medical policy, hospital formularies, and physician-led switching decisions emirate by emirate.

Three regulatory developments define the 2026 commercial landscape. Federal Decree-Law No. 38/2024, effective January 2025, consolidates marketing authorisation under the Emirates Drug Establishment (EDE), replacing MOHAP as the central federal authority. DOH's Guide to Biosimilars for Healthcare Professionals (May 2024, effective August 2024) directs treatment-naïve initiation on eight listed molecules where MOHAP-registered biosimilars exist. And SEHA's inpatient biosimilar mandate in Abu Dhabi government facilities creates a defined substitution floor that developers can plan against—while non-naïve switching remains governed by prescriber confidence and payer step therapy.

Because biologicals must be prescribed by brand name with no automatic pharmacy substitution, every switch is a physician-led, patient-consented decision. FMI stakeholder surveys flag physician reluctance on switching at roughly 43% of providers—a structural brake that slows non-naïve erosion even where policy favours biosimilars. Stakeholder pricing consensus targets the first biosimilar entrant at ≥30% below originator, compressing margin for undifferentiated products but creating a clear commercial entry threshold.

Strategic implication: Biosimilar developers should plan emirate-specific launch sequences—Abu Dhabi government channel first, then Dubai private payers—and price to the stakeholder framework. Originators retain more defence room than in Saudi NUPCO tendering on device, patient support, and indication breadth, but face steady erosion as DOH expands its listed-molecule set and insurers tighten medical policy. Leadership should stress-test share by molecule wave, naïve versus switched cohort, and emirate channel before locking UAE revenue assumptions.

Regulatory Framework
EDE, MOHAP, DHA & DOH — A Layered Authority Model

Emirates Drug Establishment (EDE) — Federal Decree-Law No. 38/2024: Effective January 2025, Federal Decree-Law No. 38/2024 establishes the EDE as the central federal authority for marketing authorisation, pharmacovigilance, and pricing oversight. MOHAP's drug registration functions transition to EDE on a phased basis. Article 7 permits biosimilar approval based on demonstrated equivalence to a reference product whose exclusivity has expired. For manufacturers with prior EMA or FDA biosimilar approval, the EDE pathway accepts international comparability packages as supporting evidence—significantly reducing standalone clinical trial burden, though UAE-specific pharmacovigilance commitments remain mandatory.

MOHAP (transitioning to EDE): All biosimilars must hold federal registration before emirate-level formulary listing. MOHAP/EDE registration remains the prerequisite for DOH approval, DHA facility access, and insurer reimbursement. During the 2025–2026 transition period, dossier submissions may route through either MOHAP or EDE depending on product category and implementing regulation status—developers should monitor EDE circulars for submission routing updates.

Department of Health — Abu Dhabi (DOH): DOH operates as the emirate-level health regulator for Abu Dhabi, issuing facility licences, formulary approvals, and clinical guidelines that carry binding force in SEHA and private Abu Dhabi facilities. DOH's August 2024 biosimilar guideline (detailed in the next section) is the most commercially significant emirate-level policy instrument in the UAE biosimilar landscape.

Dubai Health Authority (DHA): DHA regulates Dubai facilities and maintains its own formulary and clinical guideline framework. While DHA has not issued an equivalent treatment-naïve mandate, Dubai insurers and hospital pharmacy committees reference DHA clinical standards and DOH biosimilar guidance as best practice. Substitution in Dubai proceeds through insurer medical policy and hospital formulary committees rather than emirate-level directive.

Policy Deep Dive
DOH Guide to Biosimilars — August 2024 Effective Date

DOH Abu Dhabi published the Guide to Biosimilars for Healthcare Professionals in May 2024, with an effective date of August 2024. This is the most detailed emirate-level biosimilar policy in the GCC outside Saudi NUPCO procurement rules.

The guideline establishes three core principles. First, biosimilars approved by MOHAP (now EDE) and meeting EU or FDA quality standards are considered therapeutically equivalent to their reference products for treatment-naïve patients. Second, interchangeability is physician-led in consultation with the patient—automatic pharmacy substitution is explicitly prohibited for biologicals. Third, for the eight listed molecules below, healthcare professionals should initiate treatment-naïve patients on a registered biosimilar where one is available and clinically appropriate.

The eight DOH-listed molecules for treatment-naïve initiation represent the highest-volume biologic categories in Abu Dhabi government facilities:

SEHA Inpatient Mandate

SEHA (Abu Dhabi Health Services Company) operates the emirate's government hospital network. The inpatient biosimilar mandate requires SEHA facilities to use registered biosimilars for inpatients on listed molecules where clinically appropriate—creating a structural substitution floor independent of individual prescriber preference for naïve patients admitted through government channels. This mandate does not extend to outpatients already established on an originator, where physician-led switching decisions and patient consent govern the transition.

Brand-Name Prescribing & No Auto-Substitution

UAE federal pharmacy law requires biologicals to be prescribed by brand name. Pharmacists cannot automatically substitute a biosimilar for a prescribed originator at the dispensing counter—a constraint that differs materially from markets with pharmacy-level substitution (e.g., parts of Europe). Every switch requires an active prescribing decision, reinforcing the importance of physician confidence programmes and medical affairs engagement over retail or pharmacy-channel strategies.

Physician reluctance — FMI survey data: Approximately 43% of UAE healthcare providers express reluctance to switch stable patients from originator biologics to biosimilars, citing immunogenicity concerns, nocebo effects, and insufficient local real-world evidence. This reluctance is distinct from acceptance of biosimilar initiation in naïve patients—a gap that determines whether DOH policy translates into actual volume or remains aspirational for the non-naïve cohort.

Payer & Reimbursement
Thiqa, Daman, Dubai Plans & Private Insurers

Unlike Saudi Arabia's NUPCO-centred procurement, UAE biosimilar access is fragmented across government insurers, mandatory health schemes, and private medical policies—each with distinct substitution incentives and step-therapy rules.

Thiqa (Abu Dhabi basic health insurance): Thiqa covers UAE nationals in Abu Dhabi through a government-funded scheme administered under DOH oversight. Thiqa formularies align with DOH clinical guidelines, meaning the August 2024 biosimilar directive directly influences Thiqa-covered naïve patient initiation. Thiqa reimbursement rates reference stakeholder pricing frameworks that target first biosimilar entrants at ≥30% below originator list price—a threshold that shapes tender negotiations and hospital pharmacy budget impact models.

Daman (National Health Insurance Company): Daman operates as the largest health insurer in the UAE, covering both Thiqa-administered schemes and private employer plans. Daman's medical policy refresh cycles determine step-therapy requirements, prior authorisation rules, and biosimilar preference tiers for non-government insured lives. Daman policies vary by plan tier—Enhanced vs. Basic—and by emirate, creating a matrix of substitution incentives that BioNixus maps at plan level for launch sequencing.

Dubai mandatory health insurance plans: Dubai's mandatory health insurance framework (DHA-regulated) covers expatriate and private-sector populations through employer-sponsored plans. Substitution in Dubai proceeds primarily through insurer medical policy rather than emirate directive. Key Dubai insurers maintain independent formularies and prior authorisation workflows; biosimilar preference is negotiated plan by plan, with step therapy and co-pay differentials as the primary switching levers.

Self-pay and premium international plans: A significant premium segment—particularly in Dubai healthcare free zones and private hospitals—maintains originator preference through medical policy exclusions on biosimilar substitution for stable patients. This segment represents meaningful defence volume for originators but erodes gradually as DOH policy norms diffuse into private insurer best-practice guidelines.

Molecule Waves
Immunology, Oncology & G-CSF Substitution Sequencing

DOH's eight-molecule list defines the first substitution wave. Patent expiry timing, prescriber confidence, and payer policy determine pace within each therapy cluster.

Wave 1 — Immunology (adalimumab, etanercept, infliximab)

Anti-TNF biosimilars represent the highest absolute volume opportunity in the UAE, driven by rheumatoid arthritis, psoriatic arthritis, and inflammatory bowel disease prevalence in the insured population. Adalimumab leads on volume; DOH's naïve mandate creates immediate substitution floor in Abu Dhabi government facilities. Non-naïve switching in immunology faces the strongest physician reluctance (~43% FMI survey)—immunogenicity and nocebo concerns are most acute in this cluster. Device experience (auto-injector vs. prefilled syringe) remains a meaningful originator defence lever.

Wave 2 — Oncology (trastuzumab, rituximab, bevacizumab)

Oncology biosimilars grow with rising cancer burden across UAE registries. Trastuzumab substitution accelerates in hospital day-oncology units where SEHA inpatient rules and Thiqa coverage align. Rituximab and bevacizumab follow with haematology-oncology and medical oncology adoption respectively. Oncology prescribers show higher biosimilar acceptance for naïve patients than immunology specialists, but switching stable maintenance patients remains physician-gated. Budget impact models referencing ≥30% discount thresholds are the primary payer engagement tool.

Wave 3 — Supportive Care (filgrastim, pegfilgrastim)

G-CSF biosimilars are the most established substitution category globally and show the highest UAE uptake rates relative to originator share. Filgrastim and pegfilgrastim benefit from shorter treatment duration, lower nocebo risk, and established clinical equivalence data. SEHA inpatient mandate and chemotherapy protocol integration drive rapid adoption in oncology supportive-care pathways. Pricing pressure is most intense in this cluster—stakeholder frameworks expect discounts at or above the 30% threshold from first entrant.

Launch Playbook
Biosimilar Developer Launch Playbook — UAE

A sequenced commercial framework for biosimilar developers entering the UAE market, informed by BioNixus field programmes across Abu Dhabi, Dubai, and the northern emirates.

  • Phase 1 — Registration & DOH listing (Months 0–18)Submit EDE/MOHAP dossier leveraging EMA or FDA approval where available. Target DOH formulary listing concurrently with federal registration for Abu Dhabi government channel access. Map DHA facility requirements for Dubai private hospital entry. Price to ≥30% below originator from first submission to align with stakeholder framework expectations.
  • Phase 2 — Abu Dhabi government channel (Months 12–24)Prioritise SEHA inpatient formulary inclusion and Thiqa reimbursement alignment. Deploy medical affairs engagement with Abu Dhabi rheumatology, oncology, and gastroenterology KOLs. Build Arabic-language patient education materials addressing nocebo concerns. Target treatment-naïve patient initiation as the primary volume driver—DOH mandate creates structural demand without requiring non-naïve switching.
  • Phase 3 — Dubai & private payer negotiation (Months 18–36)Engage Daman medical policy teams and Dubai mandatory-plan insurers with budget impact models built on UAE-specific consumption data. Negotiate step-therapy placement and co-pay differentials that incentivise biosimilar initiation. Map hospital formulary committees at target Dubai accounts. Do not assume Abu Dhabi policy automatically transfers to Dubai—each emirate requires independent payer engagement.
  • Phase 4 — Non-naïve switching programme (Months 24–48)Launch physician confidence studies and switching protocols for stable patients only after naïve-patient volume is established. Address the ~43% physician reluctance through peer-to-peer KOL programmes, local RWE generation, and structured switching clinics. Non-naïve erosion is the long-cycle revenue driver but cannot be forced—patient consent and prescriber confidence govern every switch.
  • Phase 5 — Northern emirates & expatriate segment (Months 30–48)Extend payer negotiations to Sharjah, Ajman, and Ras Al Khaimah insurer networks. Address expatriate continuity challenges—patients established on a specific originator abroad whose switching eligibility depends on insurer medical policy and physician assessment. Map employer-sponsored plan formularies for multinational corporate accounts.
Defence Playbook
Originator Defence Playbook — UAE

Originators retain more defence room in the UAE than under Saudi NUPCO tendering, but DOH's treatment-naïve mandate and SEHA inpatient rules erode the naïve-patient pool on eight core molecules. Defence investment should concentrate on non-naïve cohorts and differentiated assets.

  • Accept naïve-patient erosion; defend non-naïve volumeDOH policy and SEHA mandates will shift treatment-naïve initiation on listed molecules regardless of originator investment. Concentrate medical affairs, patient support, and contracting spend on stable patients already established on the originator—where physician-led switching decisions and patient consent create genuine retention opportunity.
  • Device and delivery differentiationAuto-injector experience, citrate-free formulations, and patient-support programme depth remain the highest-influence retention levers in immunology—particularly adalimumab, where device switching anxiety exceeds clinical equivalence concerns. Invest in device continuity programmes for non-naïve patients facing insurer step-therapy pressure.
  • Generate UAE-specific real-world evidenceHospital consumption data and UAE patient outcomes under the originator are categorically more persuasive to DOH committees and Daman medical policy reviewers than RWE transposed from European datasets. BioNixus's patient-level consumption data from UAE hospitals enables genuine local RWE generation.
  • Map defensible accounts through consumption dataNot all accounts are worth defending at parity pricing. Hospital-level consumption data identifies where originator loyalty is strongest—premium international plans, specific KOL-led departments, and indication segments where biosimilar equivalence data is thinnest. Concentrate defence investment precisely where it generates the highest return.
  • Engage insurer medical policy proactivelyEach Daman and Dubai insurer medical policy refresh is a fresh decision point. Originators should maintain continuous payer engagement—presenting budget impact counter-models, patient support value, and real-world evidence—rather than reacting after step-therapy rules are published.
